Digital Sovereignty · Reference Architecture & Implementation

Your data is in Sydney. So why isn't it sovereign?

Residency asks where the data is stored. Sovereignty asks who can be compelled to hand it over — and what they could actually produce. ADSRA is the reference architecture that answers the second question: seven control pillars, twenty fundable capabilities, a six-level maturity model, and KRIs computed from telemetry rather than attested in a questionnaire. Assure Govern is its implementation.

Why this exists

No instrument requires "sovereignty" by name

The obligation emerges from the intersection of several — which is precisely why it keeps falling between organisational cracks. Legal owns the contract, Security owns the controls, Architecture owns the platform, and nobody owns the question a regulator will actually ask.

1 JUL 2025
APRA CPS 230 in force

Critical operations, board-approved tolerance levels, material service provider registers, and a 24-hour notification clock for tolerance-breaching disruptions.

✓ In force
JUN 2025
Privacy statutory tort live

A statutory tort for serious invasions of privacy, atop the 2022 penalty uplift: the greater of $50m, 3× the benefit, or 30% of adjusted turnover.

✓ Live
1 JUL 2026
Provider-contract transition ends

Pre-existing provider contracts captured by the earlier of renewal or 1 Jul 2026. CPS 230 targeted amendments for certain non-traditional service providers took effect the same day.

✓ Deadline passed
10 DEC 2026
Automated-decision transparency

Privacy policies must disclose substantially automated decisions that significantly affect individuals (APP 1.7–1.9) — a direct constraint on AI architecture, and the nearest hard deadline on this page.

⏳ Forthcoming
2025–26
AI policy consolidates

Guidance for AI Adoption (Oct 2025) supersedes the Voluntary AI Safety Standard; the National AI Plan (Dec 2025) relies on existing law plus an AI Safety Institute — no standalone AI Act.

The control surface

Seven questions every board should be able to answer

Each pillar is specified with control objectives (stable IDs engineering can build against), a reference pattern, indicative hyperscaler mappings, and the KRIs that evidence it. A board can fund, measure and govern a pillar — it cannot fund a vibe.

PILLAR 01

Data

Who can legally compel access to our information?

Classification with sovereignty tiers, residency and cross-border controls, tokenisation, DLP, and privacy assessment embedded in delivery rather than bolted on at the end.

PILLAR 02

Identity

Who is acting, from where, and with what privilege?

Australian-controlled IdP, Zero Trust conditional access, just-in-time elevation, session recording. Standing privilege extinct.

PILLAR 03

Cryptographic

Who controls the keys that control trust?

Customer-managed keys, HSM-backed roots of trust, and — surgically — hold-your-own-key. Encryption is the last defence, but only if you hold the keys.

PILLAR 04

Operational

Who administers production — and from which jurisdiction?

Australian operations and SOC, provider-access approval workflows, jurisdiction-aware break-glass, and exit plans that have actually been tested.

PILLAR 05

AI

Where do inference and training occur — and who governs the outputs?

Prompt gateway, policy engine, PII detection, governed RAG, model registry, output validation. Plus decision assurance: impact assessment, explainability, contestability, human oversight.

PILLAR 06

Governance

Is compliance enforced by the platform, or by a policy PDF?

Policy-as-code, pipeline gates that fail closed for critical tiers, auto-expiring exceptions. Aligned to AS ISO/IEC 42001 and the national AI-assurance framework.

PILLAR 07 · THE UNDERDEVELOPED ONE

Observability

Can we prove, audit and continuously measure sovereignty?

The least mature pillar across the Australian market and the highest-leverage investment available — because a control without evidence is treated by a regulator as a control that is absent. This is also the pillar Assure Govern implements first. Position

Sovereign models are the supply side

The model registry may point at a global frontier model or an Australian-owned sovereign model on onshore compute. Building those models is a supply-side national effort; ADSRA is the demand-side complement that governs and measures how any model is consumed.

Decision assurance is the harder half

Beyond residency, the flow evidences an algorithmic impact assessment, explainability and a contestability path for decisions that significantly affect individuals — the same axis the 10 Dec 2026 ADM-transparency obligation turns into a hard constraint.

The maturity model

From cloud adoption to autonomous sovereignty

Most Australian enterprises sit at Levels 2–3 today Position. ADSRA targets Levels 4–5 within roughly 36 months. Level 6 is the model's genuinely new contribution — and the one most open to challenge.

LEVEL 1

Cloud Adoption

Workloads migrating; controls inherited ad hoc.

LEVEL 2

Cloud Security

Essential Eight hardening; MFA; SIEM in place.

LEVEL 3

Cloud Governance

Landing zones, tagging, policy governance.

LEVEL 4

Digital Sovereignty

Classification drives residency, keys and AU operations.

LEVEL 5

AI Sovereignty

All AI via gateway and registry; RAG governance; ADM readiness.

LEVEL 6

Autonomous Sovereignty

The platform assesses and remediates its own posture.

Where the step change actually is. At Levels 1–5, sovereignty is an audit question answered quarterly, by humans, from samples. At Level 6 it becomes a continuously computed property of the platform — quarantine the workload, force the key rotation, fail inference back in-region — under human oversight proportionate to blast radius. Deliberately aspirational: pilots, not production, are the realistic 36-month target. Position

An honest ledger

Sovereignty is not free — both columns, stated plainly

Architecture papers love benefits. Investment committees trust ledgers. The managed position is not maximal sovereignty — it is tiered sovereignty, consciously priced.

Reward

  • Penalty and tort exposure avoided. Privacy penalties reach the greater of $50m, 3× the benefit, or 30% of adjusted turnover; the statutory tort adds per-incident exposure on top.
  • Regulatory speed. Pre-approved patterns collapse architecture and risk-approval cycle times — a new AI use case becomes a configuration review, not a first-principles debate.
  • Concentration risk made visible. CPS 230 registers plus sovereignty KRIs quantify what Risk Committees previously accepted implicitly.
  • The licence to deploy AI at all. Evidenced control is becoming the precondition for board approval of customer-facing AI, not a differentiator on top of it.

Risk

  • Sovereign controls tax capability. HYOK and client-side encryption break real native cloud features. Apply surgically, not universally.
  • In-region AI lags the frontier. Australian-region model availability trails global releases, sometimes by a capability generation — though sovereign model and compute options are beginning to narrow the gap. Position
  • Over-classification is self-harm. Default everything to Critical and you tax low-risk workloads and teach the organisation to route around the scheme entirely.
  • Skills are scarce. Policy-as-code, key custody and AI governance are thin markets — and thinning further as CPS 230 programmes compete for the same people.
The managed position. Four tiers (Low → Critical); the sovereignty premium spent only where obligations demand it; residual risk measured and accepted in the open. A national retailer runs its product catalogue on a global public CDN — no premium at all — because classification said it could. That is the architecture working, not the architecture being bypassed.
